<em>“To succeed in life, just work hard.”</em>

From cheesy inspirational success stories to my parents’ lectures, there probably hasn’t been a day that has passed in my life where I haven’t heard this message in one form or another. Work hard to get rich and successful. I mean, it’s so simple and intuitive that it’s practically treated as a universal truth.

But surely, we all know this world isn’t fair enough to guarantee that everyone who has worked hard is successful, nor require that all people who consider themselves successful are diligent–and this isn’t only tied to just class differences. A wealthy man with humble beginnings who lifted his family out of poverty can be considered successful, but so is the man who happened to be born with wealth and connections, and even the humble man who is complacent with living a simple life. It seems that success is something that is strictly tied to neither diligence nor wealth.

So then I began thinking: what does it mean to be successful?

The denotation of success is “the accomplishment of an aim or purpose”—an achievement to a dream. While this dream is different for each individual, such an achievement, regardless of what it is, must result in a sense of accomplishment, or satisfaction. We also know man’s most fundamental spirit is the pursuit of happiness, and thus his dream must be aligned with that as well. Then, we can use these two criteria to define success is the attainment of long-term satisfaction and happiness. 

And now, we have an interesting situation. In order to have the satisfaction of achieving a dream, we need to sacrifice our freedom and dedicate ourselves in diligence. But in order to experience happiness, we need freedom from our ambitions and obligations and expectations. Every day we make this choice. Should I finish this problem set, or should I finish binge watching a TV show? Should I clean up my room, or should I leave it for another day?

Thus, again and again we choose between diligence and liberty. Like flipping a coin, we can only roll one side, or the other. And on this coin, we gamble our lives away, struggling towards that ultimate jackpot of success. Again and again we flip this coin, sacrificing freedom for freedom, working hard so that we won’t have to work hard, unsure of which side is the “right” one, and dreaming that the next flip will be the one. And while some become complacent with winning a prize, often we will raise our bar and search for even greater wins–and still there is no success, because there is no satisfaction. That’s the nature of gambling, right?

But honestly, that’s okay with me. If I succeed, then I’ll stop striving to succeed; if I fulfill my dreams, then I’ll stop dreaming. What I’ve realized is that success, really, is just the end of a dream. Because of that, I don’t ever want to hit the jackpot, and I won’t, because for me there is no such thing. I’ll keep striving for even greater heights, chasing that evasive dream that dances just out of my reach, flipping that coin again and again. After all, the pursuit of truth has no end.

And you know, it’s a possible for a coin to land on its side. Once in a while, I’ll strike a perfect balance between carefree and ambitious, where success and happiness and satisfaction are captured in a perfect, ephemeral moment. Whether it’s finishing a problem set with classmates, sharing life philosophies while waiting for telescope exposures, learning valuable life lessons from a well-produced TV show, finding a close friend through a science competition, or reflecting on myself on a wandering nighttime stroll—

Rather than some ultimate, illusory notion of success, I think I can be content with just those moments.
