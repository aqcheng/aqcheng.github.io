there was a lost child at the county fair, legs dangling off of a huge, white-painted lawnchair the size of a small car. you were a stranger beside her as she spun the clouds floating in the blue june sky into her cotton candy dreams, smiling as wide as a bicycle wheel. 

i’d like to say that she was a sunflower in a bright summer dress. but we’re not like that, are we? we cover ourselves in black, because we fear we will melt like candles in the sun; because we hide the waxy stains of our selves in layers of shadows; because we mourn, continuously, in dark jeans and inked wings and leather jackets, the deaths of those whom we once were.

but she was alive, that day. and we were on top of the world. at the summit of the ferris wheel, there was a moment when everything disappeared but our naked souls. and you loved me, in that brief eternity. you loved me. you held me like the sky. if only we could fly into the setting sun instead of saying goodbye–i a white dress ablaze and you an ocean of kind tears;

but i stay planted to the earth. i bloom because i am still alive. i am a sunflower in a leather jacket. i am twenty years old.
