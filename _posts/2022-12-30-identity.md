today, as a college sophomore, i have a decent grasp on my identity. i am chinese-american. i am a woman (although i use that term loosely). i am queer. i am a physicist(-in-training), a double major in physics and math. 

back in high school, however, not only was i deep in the closet, i also couldn’t see myself as a physicist, or physicist-in-training, or any kind of physics or math <em>person</em>. <b>i was a girl.</b> i knew i was a girl because everywhere i went, i couldn’t see myself in anyone else. at the physics club at school, at the after school math circle, and even in the online video game spaces that i logged into late at night, it felt as though i had a giant sticky note on my back that screamed "<b>GIRL</b>". no one thought it was my fault, but nonetheless the poorly hidden condescension and occasional malicious comment gave me the dreaded feeling that for some reason, i was not supposed to be there. for a while back then, i tried to hide this metaphorical sticky note of sorts; i insisted to myself that i didn’t like girly things, that i was a girl but <em>not like the other girls</em>, because i like physics and math and video games. my own parents reinforced this idea. so despite my best efforts, the label stayed plastered on.

the problem is, and always was, about <em><b>identity</b></em>. i could never see myself in my peers, let alone the smart upperclassmen that i admired or the brilliant physicists that i’d read about. i idolized and envied those who pursued physics because i was not them, and it viscerally felt as though i could never be. i was someone who <em>tried</em> to do physics. <b>i was a girl.</b>

so, what happened between now and then? many things, including a lot of soul searching, reflections on my identity, and acknowledgement of my passion for astrophysics. progress was non-linear, but slowly, i began to identify myself. i think the primary catalyst was meeting more people like me, i.e. physicists who don't "look like physicists", whatever that means. that is, seeing more diversity in physics. yes, diversity is about being equitable, offsetting unfair disadvantages, and reducing the effects of malicious positive feedback loops. on the individual level, however, diversity is about not having to choose between who you are and who you want to be. no one should ever have to lose themselves to follow their dreams.

in the present, i feel liberated from my gender identity. being female is no longer an unwanted label; it is a label i choose to have, and it is a label i occasionally choose to not have–just as i am slowly choosing to identify myself with those who study physics, even though my mom says i don't look like them. and that's okay with me, as long as my outfits still slay...
