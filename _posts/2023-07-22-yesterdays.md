### 1.

this summer, i’ve been trying to get into the habit of reading a book in the morning, which is something i usually find myself too busy or stressed to do during the school year. i overthink a lot about what to do with my mornings because i think they kind of set the framing for how i go about the rest of my day

these past few weeks, i’ve been bouncing between a few books, one of which is *gravitation and cosmology* by steven weinberg, a textbook on, well, gravitation and cosmology. it seems like the deeper you get into physics, the more faceless the texts become. or maybe they just get older

{% include figure.html path="assets/img/07-22-23_blog/grav_and_cosmo.jpeg" class="img-fluid rounded" width=60% zoomable=true %}

since this is not my first time learning general relativity, i can now read textbooks that approach the subject more in line with its inner logic, at the cost of pedagogical accessibility. there’s something so magical about it–about scrawling along with the tensor algebra, trying to understand that the arcane symbols forming in the tip of my pen govern, simultaneously, how the earth goes around the sun, how black holes can exist as a mathematical construct, how we can hear two of them crashing into each other billions of light years away[^1]. i mean, i’m literally peering into the source code documentation for the universe in a musty old blue textbook, sitting on a big bean bag in the library basement stacks

[^1]: this is the subject of my research–[gravitational waves](https://www.ligo.caltech.edu/page/what-are-gw)!

(yes, this is the point where you can point and laugh and call me a nerd)

i’ve thought a lot (a LOT) about the question of why i love physics, especially after coming to mit, where imposter syndrome in some shape or form seems like a pretty universal experience. my answer to this probably deserves its own blogpost, but the short answer is that i think i just love it for what it is. this probably seems like a vague non-answer, so let me try to explain further

a while ago i read [a blogpost by mit blogger alan](https://mitadmissions.org/blogs/entry/trying-to-trim-the-tree-of-possible-paths/) where they introduce the framework of *completeness* for evaluating your motivations. the basic idea is that <b>a line of reasoning which is more *complete* is one in which there are less steps</b>. for example, wanting to do research because it makes you happy is more complete than wanting, say, to do quant trading because you get a lot of money and getting a lot of money allows you to buy nice things and buying nice things makes you happy. (i’ll revisit this example later.) this isn’t the first time i’ve used this kind of framework; i remember trying to reason like this when i was around 16, albeit much less formally, to try to figure out what i should do with my life. unfortunately, what i concluded was that there was a rational justification for playing video games (made me feel happy and excited) over studying physics (made me feel stressed and inept), and i spent a lot of my time doing just that because i couldn’t figure out why i shouldn’t

now, alan (and aristotle, whom they draw from) does make the distinction between “virtuous” happiness and pleasure and argues that the former is better because it is distinctly <it>human</it>. i have my doubts about this, mostly because it’s unclear to me where the line is drawn. after all, it seems like a distinctly human thing to play video games, and there are a lot of people who find genuine, ambitious meaning in gaming, especially competitive games. the only difference to me was that it was probably much more practical for me to become a physicist than a pro gamer, and so maybe there’s something to say here about <it>long-term</it> happiness. but not doing something because it’s less rationally feasible seems like an *incomplete* reason in itself; after all, projecting forward far into the future seems to require a lot of steps. also, this entire discussion only begs the question of why completeness is good in the first place. i will say though that this is a pretty crude summary of alan’s idea, which they say is a pretty crude summary of aristotle’s ideas. but usually i don’t think these ideas run that deep anyway when it comes to trying to make sense of how you feel about things

for me personally, the framework of *completeness* makes sense for two reasons. the first is that i have a lot of doubts and i often don’t have much faith in the consistency to which my brain reacts to things, so the more steps there are in a chain of reasoning, the worse that reasoning is. the second is that <b>i am staunchly an *idealist*</b>, rather than a *pragmatist*, and naturally doing something for its own sake is much more attractive to me than doing something for some extrinsic motivation. right now, studying physics does make me happy, much happier than it did in high school, as evidenced by the fact that i’ll wake in the morning and feel compelled to read a textbook in a basement. so i suppose i can employ this handy framework and say that i love physics because it makes me happy

but it’s not that simple, of course–if it was that easy no one would be stressing over what they want to do with their lives. because i don’t always know what makes me happy. because some days i’ll open gravitation and cosmology and i won’t see the magic. and some days, the prospect of even thinking about physics will fill me with dread, and i’ll stare at the ceiling and start rethinking what i want to do with my life

because all this only begs the question of *why does physics make me happy?* i think many course 8s[^2] (in physicist fashion), myself included, will go through all sorts of ends to figure this out. and while i can now come up with a few rational explanations, i think the true explanation for me is that <b>there isn’t one</b>. and there doesn’t have to be–because to whom do i owe this justification? ultimately, my reason for doing physics is *even more complete* than “it makes me happy”; <b>i want to do physics because i do</b>. it’s an attribute as fundamental as the fact that i have brown eyes or that i’m 5’10 or that i like ice cream, just a bit less tangible. all it requires is a bit of blind faith in its existence. in a way it is self-fulling, too–perhaps it is this blind faith that compels me to read a textbook in a basement first thing in the morning. how very idealistic, don’t you think?

[^2]: what mit calls a physics major

so tl;dr–i want to be a physicist, and that statement <b>exists in a vacuum</b>. there are no motivations, no chains of logic, causal or effectual. as an academic, without any justification or regard to the small *real* world in which i am embedded[^3], <b>i will do physics *for its own sake*</b>

[^3]: sometimes i like to pat myself on the back and tell myself that i’m researching for some grand reason, like the furthering of human knowledge–but that sounds just about as abstract as quant traders telling themselves they’re adding liquidity to the market, or whatever. so i’m not saying i have a moral high ground over anyone in choosing the path that i’ve chosen; in fact, it’s quite the opposite. i have a lot of respect for those who dedicate themselves to a purpose greater than self-fulfillment

### 2.

about a week and a half ago, i got on a plane and went on a field trip

this summer, i’m doing research at caltech as part of the LIGO[^4] SURF[^5] [program](https://labcit.ligo.caltech.edu/LIGO_web/students/SURF/). included in the program is a field trip to the LIGO detector in hanford, washington; that was pretty cool. included in that field trip, though, was also a trip to the hanford B reactor

[^4]: laser interferometer gravitational-wave observatory
[^5]: summer undergraduate research fellow

the B reactor is the first-ever large scale nuclear reactor, used to produce the plutonium for the trinity test and the fat man bomb in world war ii. i’m ashamed to admit that i never really bothered to learn the details of the manhattan project–i don’t think i ever really grasped just how big the scale of the project was until i was standing before a wall, a cross-section of 2000 aluminum tubes that was once filled with water to cool the reactor

{% include figure.html path="assets/img/07-22-23_blog/b_reactor_1.jpg" class="img-fluid rounded" width=30% zoomable=true %}
{% include figure.html path="assets/img/07-22-23_blog/b_reactor_2.jpg" class="img-fluid rounded" width=30% zoomable=true %}

and even that didn’t make it really sink in. it was all so… touristy. we were sat down in a big room, facing these 2000 aluminum tubes, as the guide happily explained how the reactor worked, how enrico fermi, whom we all knew from our physics classes, pioneered the chain reaction mechanism, and how water used to come through all those tubes from the pump, pushing little buttons to light up a toy model of the site–this is where the water was pumped, this is where people ate, this is where the waste was kept before they realized it was hazardous to just dump it right away in the river, and so on

after a while we got up and walked through the different rooms. they did look pretty cool, even though most of them were just filled with water pipes and pumps. but what was most unsettling to me were the signs

{% include figure.html path="assets/img/07-22-23_blog/b_reactor_signs.jpeg" class="img-fluid rounded" width=60% zoomable=true %}

the signs were in every room. dozens upon dozens of signs in a style that today only remains as an aesthetic. radioactive, keep out, keep your gear on, do not enter, and most importantly, *do not tell*. as touristy as the tour was, there was no glossing over the fact that this was the heart of a top secret military operation, one of unprecedented scale, in both science and horror, one that precisely engineered the instanteous deaths of hundreds of thousands of people, and one that would change the very perception of war itself, as it launched the world–irreversibly–into an age of looming self-annihilation

on the bus ride back from the reactor site, an old man asked the tour guide to announce to the entire bus that he had visited all 423 national parks in the united states, this being his last one. then we all clapped. i was just staring out the window, not really knowing how to feel, or what i was feeling, besides something like *ah yes, man made horrors beyond my comprehension*

except, having learned the physics, it’s not really that far beyond my comprehension anymore

### 3.

yesterday, i watched *oppenheimer*

i think i kind of had to. i mean, i just visited the B reactor. and even if i hadn’t, the name <it>oppenheimer</it> is ubiquitous in modern physics, as are many other names in the movie–einstein, fermi, bohr, feynman, hans bethe, heisenberg, and so on. that in itself was pretty incredible to see on the big screen. scientists, especially physicists, aren’t usually portrayed in movies outside of the scientist/physicist archetype, which hardly feels human, even in sci-fi movies. and of course they did dramatize physics–it’s a movie after all–but i think christopher nolan did do justice in capturing that kind of magic spark that physics and research has for me

before i get ahead of myself: if you haven’t watched it yet, <it>oppenheimer</it> is a biographical thriller about, well, oppenheimer–his career beginnings as a physicist, his tangled personal life, his involvement with the manhattan project, from the project conception to the trinity test to the deployment of the bomb on hiroshima and nagasaki, all against the backdrop of the western world in the mid-20th century, with the second world war and red scare and so on, not necessarily in chronological order. i won’t get into why this movie is so great from a cinematography standpoint. and of course, you don’t need to know any physics at all to watch and appreciate <it>oppenheimer</it>–the harrowing message of the movie is clear to any viewer. there were a lot (A LOT) of moments where i felt stunned by the rush of so many dissonant and yet resonant feelings–wonder, awe, veneration, but also pity, fear, horror, emptiness, disbelief. there’s a lot i want to say about these moments, of which there are many, but i don’t want to spoil, and i don’t think i could do justice to them in writing here anyway

to me, <it>oppenheimer</it> did not feel like just a movie. perhaps because it’s biographical, but perhaps even more because i am a physicist. because all of those people–oppenheimer, einstein, fermi, bohr, feynman, hans bethe, heisenberg–<b>they made modern physics</b>. add newton into that list, and you have the bulk of any undergrad physics curriculum. they are the people whose names live on in equations, principles, models, theories, even measurement units, on chalkboards in lecture halls across the globe a century later; they are the giants whose shoulders we stand on; they are the legendary pioneers from a golden age of physics, an age long gone, where the birth of quantum physics and relativity fundamentally changed the way we could make sense of the world

and yet they were human, all too human. oppenheimer, the father of the atomic bomb, is neither deified nor redeemed; the movie does not answer the question of whether he is redeemable. the only clear truth is that <b>he loved physics</b>. he loved physics *for its own sake*, because it was beautiful, because they were academics. when he came to berkeley and wrote down schrodinger’s equation for the first quantum physics class in the united states–a class which comprised of a single student–how did they feel? in the manhattan project, where did the physics end and where did the devastation begin? to what extent did they abstract away the moral weight of their actions in their pursuit of science? oppenheimer himself keeps repeating, almost to convince himself, that the bomb was a necessary evil, better in the america’s hands than the nazis’, only to be haunted by images of thundering noise, disintegrating flesh, after the project is realized

### 4.

i’m not sure where this all leaves me

one thing that’s on my mind is the concept of <b>abstraction</b>. an abstraction is a thing, a concept, that comes to life when you put a story to it. abstraction is something i do a lot. it’s something we all do, to some extent, in order to live our every day lives. the past exists as a concept–a schema in our brains–of key events, history books, black and white

and physics itself is an abstraction, perhaps just the most rigorous one. physics abstracts away the entire world into equations, theorems, and toy [model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model)s; all that’s left are the grand and beautiful ideas. and maybe that’s why i like it so much. it’s simpler, prettier, more tractable than the real world, both past and present. so it would seem like physics could exist in a vacuum. but it does not–it cannot. any teaching of physics opens with a discussion of its history, as does <it>gravitation and cosmology</it>:

> Physics is not a finished logical system. Rather, at any moment it spans a great confusion of ideas, some that survive like folk epics from the heroic periods of the past, and others that arise like utopian novels from our dim premonitions of a future grand synthesis… 

and of course that sounds great and all, humanity’s great conquest of truth in science, physics (in astronomy) being arguably the oldest one. but if you peel just a little beyond the surface, history becomes an ugly thing. all of modern physics, nasa, even mit itself[^6]–all of them are deeply entrenched in the cold war, world war ii, and the past beyond, and there opens a huge can of worms of questions, none of which i know how to answer

[^6]: i highly recommend watching [mit: regressions](https://www.regressions.net/), an mit student-made documentary of the history of mit, especially in the 20th century

are any of my worries or interests even significant? is the act of self-fulfillment self-serving? how many people died to pave the path i intend to walk? should i grieve–how should i grieve? i feel that i should be grateful, but whom do i thank? do i carry a burden, some kind of obligation to make the world a better place? or can i keep pursuing life in abstraction? is that even possible? and what does that even mean, anymore? does history have to be ugly, or is there something beautiful in there, too? is it dismissive to lump together the horrors and triumphs of the past as abstract features of human nature? but then, when does abstraction cross the line into crude romanticization? what is the right way to look towards both tomorrow and yesterday?

i don’t know[^7]. i don’t know what you or i should take away from all this. but i think it’s certainly something worth thinking about

[^7]: the easy answer, of course, is that none of this even matters–undoing my abstractions doesn’t make me any less ignorant than i was yesterday, and feeling morally conflicted won’t undo the suffering of the past. but that is the pragmatist’s answer, and i have decided <it>a priori</it> to reject that kind of logic
